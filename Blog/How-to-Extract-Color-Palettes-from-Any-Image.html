What Is a Color Palette and Why Does It Matter?

A color palette is simply a small collection of colors that work well together. Instead of picking colors randomly every time you design something, you choose 4–6 colors that look great together and then use those same colors every time.

Think about big brands you recognize instantly. Coca-Cola uses red and white. Spotify uses black and bright green. Instagram uses gradient purples and pinks. You know these brands partly because of their colors — even before you read any text.

The same principle applies to your personal brand, blog, Pinterest account, or small business. When all your pins, posts, and website pages use the same colors, people start to recognize your content immediately. Your feed looks professional, polished, and intentional. And that consistency builds trust with your audience.

Research from the University of Loyola Maryland shows that consistent color use increases brand recognition by up to 80%. That is huge. And it all starts with picking a good set of colors and sticking to them.

What Is a HEX Code?

A HEX code is a 6-character code that represents an exact color. Every color on a screen has its own unique HEX code. For example:

  • #FF0000 — pure red
  • #0000FF — pure blue
  • #FFFFFF — white
  • #000000 — pure black

HEX codes are used in web design (CSS), Canva, Figma, Adobe tools, and almost every design application. When you have the exact HEX code of a color, you can reproduce that exact color anywhere — on your website, in a Canva template, in a banner, in an Instagram graphic. No guessing, no trying to match by eye.

When you extract a color palette from a photo, you are getting the HEX codes of that photo's most dominant colors. Then you can use those exact colors consistently across your entire brand.

Why Extract Colors from a Photo Instead of Just Picking Them?

You might be wondering — why not just choose any colors you like? Why extract them from a photo?

The answer is that nature and professional photography naturally produce colors that look beautiful together. A photographer does not randomly choose what to include in a shot. They capture real light, real scenes, and real color relationships that our eyes naturally find pleasing.

When you extract a palette from a beautiful nature photo, you are inheriting that natural color harmony. The colors already look great together because they existed together in the real world. This is why designers often start projects by finding an inspirational image and pulling colors from it — it is a shortcut to a beautiful, balanced palette.

On top of that, extracting colors from your own brand photos — your product shots, your nature backgrounds, your lifestyle images — means your color palette is directly connected to your visual content. Everything ties together naturally.

Understanding What You Get — Reading Your Color Palette

After you extract a palette, you will have 5–8 HEX codes. But not all of these colors should be used equally in your designs. Here is a simple way to think about how to assign roles to your palette colors:

The 60-30-10 Color Rule

Professional designers use a simple guideline called the 60-30-10 rule to make sure their color palettes look balanced rather than overwhelming:

  • 60% — Your main background color: This is the color that takes up most of the space. Usually a neutral tone — light, soft, or white. Use it for page backgrounds, large sections, and content areas.
  • 30% — Your secondary color: This supports the main color and creates variety. Use it for cards, sidebars, headers, and mid-level design elements.
  • 10% — Your accent color: This is the color that stands out and grabs attention. Use it sparingly — for buttons, links, highlights, borders, and the most important elements you want people to notice.

How to Use Your Palette in CSS for Your Website

If you have a website, the best way to use your brand colors is to define them as CSS variables. This means you only need to set your colors in one place, and they apply everywhere across your whole site. Here is how simple it is:

:root {
  --color-main: #F5F0E8;   /* 60% — main background */
  --color-secondary: #2D5016;  /* 30% — headers and cards */
  --color-accent: #E07B39;    /* 10% — buttons and links */
  --color-text: #1c1c1e;     /* body text */
}

Once these variables are set, you use them anywhere in your CSS like this:

body { background: var(--color-main); }
h1, h2 { color: var(--color-secondary); }
.btn { background: var(--color-accent); color: white; }
a { color: var(--color-accent); }

This approach means if you ever want to update your brand colors later, you just change the HEX codes in one place and the change applies across your whole website instantly.

Frequently Asked Questions

What types of images give the best color palettes?
Nature photography consistently produces the most beautiful and balanced color palettes because natural light and scenes contain harmonious color relationships. Sunsets, forests, oceans, flower fields, and mountains all produce gorgeous palettes. Avoid images that are mostly one single color — they will produce a boring palette with little variety. Also avoid images with very harsh artificial lighting, which can produce unnatural color readings.
Can I use the extracted colors on my website and products commercially?
Yes, absolutely. Colors themselves cannot be copyrighted. The extracted HEX color codes are free for you to use for any personal or commercial purpose — on your website, in products, in marketing materials, or anywhere else. Just make sure you have the right to use the source photo itself (use free stock photos or your own photography).
How many colors should I use in my brand palette?
Most successful brands use 3–5 colors consistently. The 60-30-10 rule suggests three main roles: a dominant background color, a secondary structural color, and one accent/highlight color. The extractor may give you 6–8 colors — choose your 3–5 favorites and use those consistently. Trying to use all 8 colors equally in every design tends to make things look chaotic and unprofessional.
Is my image stored or uploaded anywhere when I use the Color Extractor?
No — never. Toolkit Pro's Color Palette Extractor processes everything entirely in your browser using JavaScript. Your image never leaves your device and is never uploaded to any server. This means your photos are completely private. It also means the tool works even if your internet connection is slow, because no data is being sent back and forth to a server.
What is the difference between HEX, RGB, and HSL color formats?
They are just different ways of writing the same color. HEX codes like #FF6B35 are the most widely used in web design and design tools. RGB (like rgb(255, 107, 53)) is also common in CSS. HSL (hue, saturation, lightness) is useful for making systematic color adjustments. For most creators and designers, HEX codes are the most practical format — they are accepted everywhere and easy to copy-paste. Toolkit Pro outputs HEX codes, which you can use directly in Canva, CSS, Figma, and most other tools.
How do I know if two colors from my palette go well together?
Colors extracted from the same photo almost always work well together because they existed in the same real-world scene. That is actually the biggest benefit of palette extraction versus picking random colors — natural color harmony is built in. If you want to check contrast specifically (important for text readability), you can test any two HEX codes on WebAIM Contrast Checker (webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker). For text on a background, aim for a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1.
