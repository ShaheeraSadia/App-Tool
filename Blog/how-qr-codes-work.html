What Is a QR Code?

QR stands for Quick Response. A QR code is a type of barcode — but instead of the simple straight lines you see on food packaging in a supermarket, a QR code is a square pattern made up of small black and white squares arranged in a grid.

The big difference between a regular barcode and a QR code is how much information they can hold. A regular barcode can only hold about 20 numbers — enough for a product price or stock number. A QR code can hold up to 7,089 numbers, or about 4,296 letters and characters. That is enough space to store a full website address, a phone number, a Wi-Fi password, a business card, a short paragraph of text, or even a small poem.

QR codes were invented in 1994 by a Japanese company called Denso Wave. They were originally created to track car parts moving through factories — workers could scan them quickly with a handheld reader to identify parts without typing anything. The "Quick Response" name comes from how fast they can be scanned compared to typing information manually.

For most of us in 2026, QR codes are something we use with our smartphones every day — scanning menus, making payments, connecting to Wi-Fi, and visiting websites without typing anything.

How Does a QR Code Store Information?

This is the part that most people find interesting once they understand it. The information inside a QR code is not stored as visible text — it is encoded into the pattern of black and white squares.

Think of it like Morse code. In Morse code, dots and dashes represent letters and numbers. In a QR code, black squares and white squares (spaces) represent data in a similar way. Each combination of black and white squares in the grid corresponds to a specific piece of information.

The information is laid out in a very specific arrangement. Different areas of the QR code grid serve different purposes — some areas store the actual data, some areas help the scanner find and orient the code, and some areas contain backup copies of the data in case part of the code gets damaged or covered.

When you create a QR code for a URL like https://app-tool.vercel.app, a QR code generator converts those letters and symbols into a binary pattern — a series of 1s and 0s. Then it arranges those 1s and 0s as black and white squares in the grid. The resulting pattern is your QR code.

The Parts of a QR Code — What Each Area Does

A QR code is not just a random pattern. It has specific zones that each serve a purpose. Here is what each part of a QR code does:

1

Position Markers

The three large squares in three corners of the code. These tell your phone where the code is and how to orient it. Without them, your phone could not find the code in the camera view.

2

Alignment Pattern

A smaller square near the bottom-right corner. It helps your phone read the code correctly even if the surface is curved — like a bottle or a can.

3

Timing Pattern

Alternating black and white lines between the position markers. These define the grid spacing and help the scanner count how many squares are in each row and column.

4

Data Modules

The main body of the code filled with small black and white squares. This is where your actual information — website URL, text, phone number — is stored as a binary pattern.

5

Error Correction

Backup copies of the data distributed through the code. If part of your QR code is damaged, torn, or covered by a sticker, your phone can still read it because of this built-in backup system.

6

Quiet Zone

The white border around the whole code. This empty space separates the code from surrounding text or images, telling the scanner where the code starts and ends.

How Does Your Phone Read a QR Code?

When you point your phone camera at a QR code, something quite clever happens in under a second. Here is the whole process broken down into simple steps:

1

Camera captures the image

Your phone's camera takes a photo of whatever you are pointing it at. It is continuously analyzing the image it sees, looking for the specific three-corner pattern that marks a QR code.

2

Phone finds the three position markers

The QR reader software in your phone identifies the three large squares in the corners of the code. This tells it exactly where the code is in the image and which way it is oriented — even if the code is tilted or partially at an angle.

3

Phone maps out the grid

Using the position markers and timing patterns, the phone maps a precise grid over all the small squares in the code. It identifies each cell in the grid as either black (1) or white (0).

4

Error correction checks for damage

The phone checks if any part of the code is damaged or unreadable. If so, it uses the built-in error correction data to fill in the gaps and reconstruct the full data. Up to 30% of a QR code can be missing or damaged and it will still work.

5

Data is decoded into readable text

The binary pattern of black and white squares is translated back into the original text — a URL, phone number, Wi-Fi password, or whatever information was encoded. This all takes under one second.

6

Phone shows you the result

A notification appears on your screen with the decoded information. If it is a URL, you can tap to open it in your browser. If it is a phone number, you can tap to call. If it is a Wi-Fi password, your phone connects automatically.

Good to know: You do not need to download a special app to scan QR codes. Every modern smartphone — iPhone and Android — can scan QR codes directly from the built-in Camera app. Just open your camera, point it at the code, and a notification will appear. No extra app needed.

Static vs Dynamic QR Codes — What Is the Difference?

There are two types of QR codes and it is important to understand the difference before you create one:

Static QR Codes

A static QR code has the information permanently encoded into the pattern. Whatever URL or text you put in when you created it stays there forever. You cannot change it later.

Advantages: Free to create and use, works forever with no subscription, no servers involved — the data is directly in the code itself.

Disadvantages: If you want to change the destination URL later, you have to create a brand new QR code and reprint all your materials. You also cannot track how many times the code has been scanned.

Dynamic QR Codes

A dynamic QR code encodes a short redirect URL. When someone scans it, they are sent to a server which then redirects them to the real destination. You can change the destination URL at any time — without changing the code itself.

Advantages: You can update the destination URL anytime, you can track scan statistics (how many scans, when, where, what device), much better for marketing campaigns.

Disadvantages: Usually requires a paid subscription service. If the service goes offline, your QR codes stop working.

Which should you use? For most creators, bloggers, and small businesses, static QR codes are perfectly fine and completely free. If your URL is not going to change — like your website homepage, your Pinterest profile, or your business contact page — use a free static QR code. Tips for Making Sure Your QR Code Works Every Time

A QR code that does not scan properly is worse than no QR code at all. Here are the most important tips to make sure your QR code is always scannable:

  • Keep enough contrast. QR codes need high contrast between the dark squares and the light background. Black on white is the most reliable combination.
  • Do not make it too small. For print materials, QR codes should be at least 2 cm × 2 cm (about 0.8 inches).
  • Keep the quiet zone clear. The white border around the QR code must stay empty.
  • Always test before printing. Scan your QR code with multiple phones before printing any materials.
  • Use a short URL. Shorter URLs create simpler QR codes with fewer squares.
  • Save as PNG, not JPEG. PNG keeps the squares crisp and clear at any size.

Frequently Asked Questions About QR Codes

Do QR codes expire?
Static QR codes (like the ones Toolkit Pro generates for free) never expire. The information is encoded directly into the pattern and does not rely on any external server. As long as the QR code image is intact and the destination URL is still active, the code will work forever.
Can QR codes be hacked or contain viruses?
A QR code itself cannot contain a virus — it just contains text or a URL. However, that URL could potentially lead to a malicious website. Always check the URL that appears on your screen after scanning before you tap to open it.
How small can I print a QR code and still have it scan?
The minimum practical size for a QR code in print is about 2 cm × 2 cm (approximately 0.8 inches square). Below this size, the small modules become too tiny for most smartphone cameras to resolve clearly.
Can I add my logo to a QR code?
Yes — this is called a branded QR code. Because QR codes have built-in error correction, you can place a small logo in the center of the code without affecting readability. Always test a logo QR code thoroughly across different devices before using it in print materials.
Can QR codes be scanned from a screen?
Yes, absolutely. You can scan a QR code displayed on a computer screen, tablet screen, or even another phone screen with no problems.
